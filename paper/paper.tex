\begin{abstract}
Computing the molecular hydrogen number density \(\nHH\) self-consistently
in three-dimensional interstellar-medium (ISM) simulations requires
solving stiff non-equilibrium chemistry coupled to radiative transfer, a
cost that limits the resolution and parameter coverage of modern
calculations. We evaluate machine-learning surrogates that predict
\(\logn\) cell-by-cell on a controlled suite of seven \(128^3\) UV-only
chemistry simulations spanning \(\Gzero \in \{0.1, \dots, 6.4\}\) Habing
(14.7 million cells), under leave-one-\(\Gzero\)-out cross-validation:
each fold withholds an entire simulation at a UV field strength absent
from training. Alongside the usual log-space \(\Rsq\) we evaluate the
predictions as physical fields, decomposing the RMSE into bias and
scatter and tracking the total H\(_2\) mass budget and phase-conditional
accuracy. These metrics expose what \(\Rsq\) hides: models within 0.03 of
each other in \(\Rsq\) differ by factors of several in recovered H\(_2\)
mass. Multi-scale box-filter neighbourhood features (kernel widths 3, 5,
7 voxels; computed in \(\approx\)30\,s by separable convolution) reduce
the RMSE of gradient-boosted trees from 0.49 to 0.37\dex{} by acting on
the scatter term, while a Ridge-stacked tree--MLP ensemble minimises
squared error at the price of a systematic \(+0.3\dex\) offset that
compounds to a \(\times\)1.7 mass over-prediction. Density-weighted
training and a two-parameter, mass-weighted bias calibration fitted on
training cubes alone repair the budget: the final pipeline reaches
RMSE \(= 0.25\dex\), \(\Rsq = 0.990\), and a total H\(_2\) mass within 9 per
cent of truth on every fold, with molecular-phase \(\Rsq = 0.99\).
Ablating the solver-provided self-shielding factor costs little in
aggregate (\(\Rsq\) \(0.985 \rightarrow 0.964\)) but most of the
molecular-phase fidelity (molecular \(\Rsq\) \(0.991 \rightarrow 0.840\)).
A 3D U-Net trained on the same suite is the best interpolator in the
comparison (interior-fold \(\Rsq = 0.992\), RMSE \(0.22\dex\)) but degrades
at both extrapolation boundaries (\(\Rsq = 0.88\)--0.90) and over-predicts
the H\(_2\) mass of the hardest fold by more than two orders of magnitude,
a failure mode the global calibration cannot repair. Within-cube sampling experiments --- with cell-local inputs available
everywhere but labels and neighbourhood context restricted to the
sample --- show that reconstruction quality is controlled by sampling
geometry rather than sample volume: 10 per cent uniformly random
labelled coverage reconstructs the field nearly losslessly, while a
contiguous half-cube retains \(\Rsq = 0.86\) but corrupts the total-mass
budget by up to two orders of magnitude.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
The formation of molecular hydrogen on dust-grain surfaces
\citep{Cazaux2004} and its photodissociation by Lyman--Werner UV photons
\citep{Habing1968,Draine1996} together determine the molecular content
of the interstellar medium (ISM). Because H\(_2\) is the dominant
molecular reservoir and the direct precursor of CO and the other
molecular tracers used to infer star-formation rates
\citep{Narayanan2012}, the H\(_2\) abundance sets the initial conditions
for the star-formation sequence. Computing it self-consistently within a
three-dimensional hydrodynamics simulation requires integrating a stiff
system of non-equilibrium chemistry equations at every grid cell,
tracking the competition between H\(_2\) formation on grain surfaces, UV
photodissociation in the Lyman--Werner band (11.2--13.6\,eV), and the
H\(_2\) self-shielding that occurs when H\(_2\) columns attenuate their own
dissociating radiation \citep{Wolcott-Green2011}. In simulations with
\(10^6\)--\(10^7\) cells this chemistry solver can dominate the compute
budget, and covering a wide UV-parameter range at full resolution is
often infeasible \citep{Grassi2014}.

Two families of shortcuts exist. The older one replaces the solver with
analytic or fitted formulae for the atomic-to-molecular transition,
derived from photodissociation-region theory or calibrated on
simulations \citep{Krumholz2009,Gnedin2011,Sternberg2014,Bialy2016};
these are fast and interpretable, but each is tied to the equilibrium
assumptions, geometry, and averaging scale for which it was derived. The
newer family trains machine-learning (ML) surrogates on solver outputs:
\citet{deMijolla2019} emulate UCLCHEM abundances for molecular-line
modelling, \citet{Palud2023} emulate the Meudon photodissociation-region
code for observational inference, \citet{Holdship2021} advance a
thermochemical state through a time-step with the Chemulator network,
\citet{Grassi2022} compress a chemical network onto a learned
low-dimensional manifold, and, closest to this work, \citet{Branca2024}
train DeepONet emulators on KROME \citep{Grassi2014} integrations to
reproduce the \emph{time evolution} of a nine-species ISM photochemistry
network at a single point, reaching mean relative errors below 3 per
cent with a \(\sim\)128\(\times\) speed-up over the stiff ODE solver;
\citet{Janssen2026} benchmark such chemistry surrogates systematically
across architectures and reaction networks. All of these emulators are
zero-dimensional (or, for the PDR emulator, one-dimensional): they map a
local state or a small parameter vector to a local output and treat
every cell or sightline independently. The present work addresses a complementary question
that a zero-dimensional emulator cannot: how should a per-cell surrogate
represent the \emph{non-local} information --- principally UV shielding
by surrounding gas \citep[cf.][]{Glover2007,Glover2010} --- that couples
a cell's equilibrium H\(_2\) density to its three-dimensional environment,
and how well do the resulting models generalise to radiation-field
strengths absent from training?

Three properties of the problem shape the answer. First, \(\nHH\) in our
simulation suite spans roughly 16 orders of magnitude (from
\(\sim\)\(10^{-12}\) to \(\sim\)\(10^{4}\)\,cm\(^{-3}\)), so all modelling is done
in \(\log_{10}\) space. Second, the atomic-to-molecular transition is
sharp: the equilibrium abundance changes by orders of magnitude across a
small range of shielding column. Third, and most importantly, a cell's
chemical state is not a function of its local primitive variables alone;
whether it is shielded from dissociating UV depends on the column of gas
and dust between it and the radiation source.

A volumetric convolutional neural network (CNN) is the natural
modelling choice for the non-locality problem: a 3D U-Net
\citep{Ronneberger2015,Cicek2016} processing the whole volume can in
principle learn shielding-like aggregations through its receptive field.
But when the training set contains only a handful of simulation cubes at
discrete parameter values, the volumetric sample count is tiny compared
with the parameter count of even a small U-Net, and the behaviour of
such models on out-of-distribution parameter values is not well
characterised. We compare this learned approach against a deliberately
simple engineered alternative: precomputed multi-scale box-filter
averages of all input fields, appended to the per-cell feature vector of
tabular models (gradient-boosted trees and multilayer perceptrons).

A second theme of the paper is that \emph{how} a surrogate is scored
determines what one learns about it. A predicted \(\nHH\) field is a
physical object: downstream applications integrate it into masses and
column densities, split it into diffuse and molecular phases, and
compare its statistics to observations. We therefore evaluate every
model not only with the log-space \(\Rsq\) conventional in this
literature, but with a metric hierarchy designed around those uses ---
RMSE decomposed into bias and scatter, the total H\(_2\) mass budget,
phase-conditional errors, and distributional distances
(Section~\ref{sec:metrics}). These metrics change the conclusions: the
minimum-squared-error ensemble carries a systematic \(+0.3\dex\) offset,
invisible at the precision R\(^2\) is usually quoted, that compounds to a
\(\times\)1.7 error in total H\(_2\) mass. We treat that bias as a
first-class modelling target, comparing two designed calibration
functionals --- one that centres the cell-mean log error and one that
centres the mass budget --- fitted only on training-cube quantities.

This paper makes five contributions. (i) We define a stringent
out-of-distribution evaluation protocol --- leave-one-\(\Gzero\)-out
cross-validation over a controlled seven-simulation UV-parameter sweep,
with every model scored on the same native \(128^3\) cells --- together
with a physics-weighted metric hierarchy, and show that both are needed:
random cell-level splits would inflate every conclusion, and \(\Rsq\)
alone would miss the dominant error mode. (ii) We show that multi-scale
spatial neighbourhood features deliver a consistent accuracy gain to
tabular models at negligible cost, and that the gain is purely a
scatter reduction --- non-local context buys precision, not calibration.
(iii) We identify the residual bias of the stacked ensemble as a smooth
function of the UV field strength and repair it with a two-parameter
calibration fitted on training cubes alone, comparing the cell-mean and
mass-weighted variants; the final pipeline reaches RMSE \(= 0.25\dex\),
\(\Rsq = 0.990\), and a total H\(_2\) mass within 9 per cent of truth on
every fold. (iv) Because one input feature --- the H\(_2\) self-shielding
factor \(\fshield\) --- is itself produced by the chemistry solver, we
quantify the surrogate's dependence on it with a dedicated ablation,
finding that the aggregate cost is modest (\(\Rsq\)
\(0.985 \rightarrow 0.964\) for the adopted configuration in the
comparison protocol of Table~\ref{tab:fh2}) but that the
molecular-phase accuracy bears most of it (molecular \(\Rsq\)
\(0.991 \rightarrow 0.840\)). (v) We characterise when the volumetric
approach is and is not competitive: the 3D U-Net is the best
interpolator in the comparison but degrades sharply at both
extrapolation boundaries, over-predicts the H\(_2\) mass of the hardest
fold by more than two orders of magnitude, and carries roughly two
orders of magnitude higher per-model training cost.

The paper is structured as follows. Section~\ref{sec:data} describes the
data. Section~\ref{sec:protocol} defines the evaluation protocol,
metrics, and reproducibility measures. Section~\ref{sec:methods}
describes the feature engineering, models, and calibration.
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the main comparison, the mass-budget
analysis, the \(\fshield\) ablation, the volumetric baseline, and the
error analysis of the final model. Section~\ref{sec:supp} reports
supplementary experiments on cross-\(\Gzero\) transfer and within-cube
sampling geometry. Sections~\ref{sec:discussion} and~\ref{sec:conc} give
the discussion and conclusions.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Simulation suite}
\label{sec:suite}

The data set consists of seven three-dimensional uniform-grid
simulations from a UV-only chemistry suite. Each simulation occupies a
\(128 \times 128 \times 128\) grid of 2,097,152 cells, evolving identical
turbulent ISM initial conditions under a spatially uniform, time-steady
UV radiation field of strength \(\Gzero\) in Habing units
\citep{Habing1968}. The seven runs differ in their imposed \(\Gzero\)
alone, sharing the same initial turbulent realisation and grid geometry;
the suite is a controlled UV-parameter sweep, not a set of independent
realisations. The evolved snapshots nonetheless differ in their
hydrodynamic state as well as their chemistry --- the median gas
temperature rises from \(\approx\)\(1.0\times10^{3}\)\,K at \(\Gzero = 0.1\)
to \(\approx\)\(1.1\times10^{4}\)\,K at \(\Gzero = 6.4\) as UV heating
strengthens, with corresponding shifts in the density and velocity
fields --- while the large-scale structure remains strongly correlated
across the suite. The \(\Gzero\)
values span a 64-fold range on a geometric sequence,
\(\Gzero \in \{0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.8, 1.6, 3.2, 6.4\}\), and the snapshots
represent quasi-equilibrium chemical states. The UV field illuminates
each cube along the \(+z\) axis, which breaks the full octahedral grid
symmetry and dictates the augmentation strategy of
Section~\ref{sec:cnn}. The dominant chemical processes in the suite are
H\(_2\) formation on grain surfaces \citep{Cazaux2004} and Lyman--Werner
photodissociation \citep{Draine1996} attenuated by H\(_2\) self-shielding
computed along the illumination axis \citep{Wolcott-Green2011}. The
total data set contains 14,680,064 cells.

We use this suite as a controlled testbed for surrogate methodology: the
single-parameter design isolates the question of generalisation across
the UV field strength from all other sources of variation. The
corresponding limitation --- that generalisation across turbulent
realisations, metallicities, or density regimes is untested --- is
discussed in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}. A complete description of
the simulation code, chemical network, and initial conditions
(box size, resolution, driving, metallicity, dust model) is beyond the
scope of this methods-focused paper and must accompany any future
application of the surrogate to scientific inference; we state
explicitly which conclusions depend only on the internal structure of
the data (all of them) and which depend on the physical realism of the
simulations (none directly).

\subsection{Physical fields}
\label{sec:fields}

Each cell carries 18 raw fields: three integer grid coordinates
(excluded from model input), the target \(\nHH\), and 15 input features
summarised in Table~\ref{tab:features}. Fields spanning many orders of
magnitude are log-transformed,
\begin{equation}
  x_\ell = \log_{10}(x + \varepsilon), \qquad \varepsilon = 10^{-30},
  \label{eq:logtransform}
\end{equation}
where \(\varepsilon\) guards against \(\log(0)\) without affecting any
physically realised value (the smallest non-zero \(\nHH\) in the suite is
\(\sim\)\(5\times10^{-12}\)\,cm\(^{-3}\) and the smallest \(\fshield\) is
\(\sim\)\(10^{-24}\)).

The H\(_2\) self-shielding factor \(\fshield\) merits a precise statement.
It is the attenuation factor applied to the Lyman--Werner
photodissociation rate, computed by the chemistry solver from the H\(_2\)
column between each cell and the UV source. It is not algebraically
derived from the cell's own \(\nHH\) --- we verified directly in the data
that \(\fshield\) does not equal the local molecular fraction
\(2\nHH/(n_{\mathrm{H}} + 2\nHH)\) for any cell, and that cells with
nearly identical local \(\nHH\) carry \(\fshield\) values differing by
orders of magnitude depending on their depth within the cloud --- but as
a column integral over the very field being predicted it imports
target information through the solver, and it is an output of the very
calculation the surrogate aims to replace. In a deployment scenario
where the solver has not been run, \(\fshield\) would have to be
estimated, e.g.\ from a dust-extinction proxy or an approximate
radiative-transfer sweep. Its contribution to surrogate accuracy is
therefore quantified separately (Section~\ref{sec:ablation_fh2}) rather
than assumed, and the configuration without it is the one we consider
deployable. The dust-extinction proxy \texttt{ext} is also a column-type
quantity, but unlike \(\fshield\) it is computable from the density field
alone without chemical information.

\subsection{Target distribution}
\label{sec:target}

Figure~\ref{fig:histograms} shows the distribution of \(\logn\) across
\(\Gzero\). The target spans \(\approx\)16 orders of magnitude, from
\(\sim\)\(10^{-12}\)\,cm\(^{-3}\) in UV-exposed gas to
\(\sim\)\(10^{4}\)\,cm\(^{-3}\) in shielded cores, leaving linear-space
regression severely ill-conditioned and motivating log-space modelling
throughout. At \(\Gzero = 0.1\) the distribution is bimodal --- a
UV-exposed population near \(\logn \approx -10\) and a self-shielded
molecular population near \(\logn \approx 0\) --- and the per-cube mean
falls from \(-3.1\) at \(\Gzero = 0.1\) to \(-7.2\) at \(\Gzero = 6.4\) as
photodissociation becomes dominant. This strong dependence of the
marginal target distribution on \(\Gzero\) is what makes
leave-one-\(\Gzero\)-out evaluation demanding: each held-out cube has a
target distribution shifted relative to everything seen in training.

\section{Evaluation protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}

\subsection{Leave-one-\texorpdfstring{\(\Gzero\)}{G0}-out cross-validation}
\label{sec:cv}

All headline results use leave-one-\(\Gzero\)-out seven-fold
cross-validation: each fold withholds one entire cube
(\(\approx\)2.1\,M cells) and trains on the remaining six
(\(\approx\)12.6\,M cells). Random cell-level splits would place cells
from every cube --- hence every \(\Gzero\) and the single shared
turbulence realisation --- in both training and test sets; under that
protocol a pointwise gradient-boosted tree already exceeds
\(\Rsq = 0.99\), telling us nothing about generalisation. The intended
application is prediction at a UV field strength that has not been
simulated, and leave-one-\(\Gzero\)-out measures exactly that. Every model
--- tabular and volumetric alike --- is evaluated on the same native
\(128^3\) cells of the held-out cube.

The two boundary folds require genuine extrapolation: no training data
exist below \(\Gzero = 0.1\) or above \(\Gzero = 6.4\). The five interior
folds require interpolation between bracketing cubes. Empirically, which
folds are hardest depends on the model family (Table~\ref{tab:main}).
Gradient-boosted trees lose most accuracy at the upward fold
(\(\Gzero = 6.4\)): tree leaves are piecewise-constant, so in the
\(\log\Gzero\) coordinate --- the only input that identifies the regime
--- their splits saturate at the training range and the model cannot
extrapolate the trend. The 3D U-Net degrades at both boundaries. The MLP
and the ensembles built on it are instead weakest at the interior fold
\(\Gzero = 0.8\), showing that \(\Gzero\)-extrapolation is not the only
failure mode the protocol exposes.

\subsection{Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

A predicted \(\nHH\) field feeds downstream uses --- mass budgets, phase
statistics, synthetic observations --- that are sensitive to different
error properties, so we score every model with a fixed metric hierarchy
rather than a single statistic. All metrics are computed in \(\logn\)
space over the \(128^3\) cells of the held-out cube and aggregated over
the seven folds as mean \(\pm\) fold standard deviation; the seven folds,
not the 14.7 million cells, are the inferential unit, and model-ranking
statements are checked for per-fold consistency.

\emph{Primary metrics.} The root-mean-square error (dex), decomposed
exactly into a systematic and a random part,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{RMSE}^2 = b^2 + s^2, \qquad
  b = \langle \hat{y} - y \rangle, \quad
  s = \mathrm{std}(\hat{y} - y),
  \label{eq:rmse}
\end{equation}
where \(b\) is the mean residual (bias) and \(s\) the residual scatter; and
the total H\(_2\) mass ratio
\begin{equation}
  \Mratio = \frac{\sum_i 10^{\hat{y}_i}}{\sum_i 10^{y_i}},
  \label{eq:massratio}
\end{equation}
which on a uniform grid with equal cell volumes equals the ratio of
predicted to true total H\(_2\) mass. These two answer the question
``can the predicted densities be trusted as physical values?'': a bias
of \(b\) dex that is invisible to variance-normalised statistics
multiplies the mass budget by up to \(10^{b}\).

\emph{Secondary metrics.} The log-space coefficient of determination
\begin{equation}
  \Rsq = 1 - \frac{\sum_i (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}{\sum_i (y_i - \bar{y})^2}
  \label{eq:r2}
\end{equation}
is retained for comparability with the emulator literature, with one
caveat used throughout: because its denominator is the held-out fold's
target variance, per-fold \(\Rsq\) rewards models that win high-variance
folds, and model rankings under mean \(\Rsq\) can invert relative to
pooled squared error (Section~\ref{sec:results_main} contains a concrete
example). We also report the fraction of cells with absolute residual
below 0.1 and 0.3\dex{} (\(f_{0.1}\), \(f_{0.3}\)); phase-conditional
\(\Rsq\) and bias for the molecular (\(y > -4\)) and diffuse (\(y \le -4\))
populations, the threshold lying in the gap between the two modes of
Figure~\ref{fig:histograms}; and the mass-weighted mean absolute error
\(\mathrm{MAE_{mw}}\) (weights \(\propto 10^{y}\)), which measures accuracy
where the H\(_2\) mass resides. The phase-conditional conclusions do not
depend on the exact split: moving the threshold anywhere in \([-5, -3]\)
changes the deployed pipeline's cross-fold molecular-phase \(\Rsq\)
smoothly and monotonically from 0.976 to 0.995 and its diffuse-phase
\(\Rsq\) from 0.88 to 0.93, with the per-fold pattern unchanged.

\emph{Supporting metrics.} The 1-D Wasserstein distance \(W_1\) (dex)
between the true and predicted marginal distributions of \(\logn\)
measures distributional realism independently of per-cell pairing; and a
skill score
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{skill} = 1 - \mathrm{MSE}/\mathrm{MSE_{ref}},
  \label{eq:skill}
\end{equation}
with the pointwise 15-feature XGBoost model as the reference, replaces
\(\Rsq\)'s fold-mean denominator with a meaningful baseline.

Linear-space quantities require one numerical guard: before
exponentiation, predictions are clipped to within \(1\dex\) of the
held-out fold's true range, preventing a handful of outlier cells from
dominating \(\Mratio\) (and the linear-space \(\Rsq\), which we report only
in the field-level analysis of Section~\ref{sec:per_fold}). For the
well-calibrated tabular models the clip is essentially never active;
for models that over-predict beyond the clip the reported \(\Mratio\) is a
\emph{lower bound} on the violation, and we flag those cases
explicitly. The MLP's own predictions are additionally clamped to the
training-target range \(\pm 2\dex\) as part of the model definition
(Section~\ref{sec:models}); this uses training information only.

\subsection{Reproducibility}
\label{sec:repro}

Every experiment is logged to a timestamped JSON file containing the
full run configuration (feature list, hyperparameters, random seed,
software environment) and per-fold metrics. Random seeds are fixed per
fold (a global seed plus the fold index). The data files are
fingerprinted by a SHA-256 checksum recorded in each log, so any change
to the input data is detectable. Every result quoted in this paper is
traceable to an archived log, and the complete pipeline runs end-to-end
on one consumer GPU. The gradient-boosted-tree results are bit-identical
across repeated runs of the pipeline; the neural models are seeded and
reproducible on fixed hardware.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Multi-scale spatial neighbourhood features}
\label{sec:spatial}

The dominant non-local effect on \(\nHH\) is UV attenuation by surrounding
gas. To give pointwise models access to spatial context without training
a volumetric network, we precompute uniform box-filter averages of all
15 input fields. For each feature \(f\) and cube, the tabular values are
reshaped to a \(128^3\) volume and filtered at kernel widths
\(k \in \{3, 5, 7\}\) voxels:
\begin{equation}
  \bar{f}_{\bm{i},k} = \frac{1}{k^3}
    \sum_{\bm{j} \in \mathcal{N}_k(\bm{i})} f_{\bm{j}},
  \label{eq:boxfilter}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{N}_k(\bm{i})\) is the \(k \times k \times k\) neighbourhood
centred on \(\bm{i}\) (kernel volumes of 27, 125, and 343 voxels);
neighbourhoods are reflected at the box faces. The box
filter is separable, so the cost is \(\mathcal{O}(N)\) per scale
independent of \(k\); computing all 45 filtered fields for all seven cubes
takes \(\approx\)30\,s. The filtered values are concatenated with the 15
local features into a 60-dimensional vector:
\begin{equation}
  \bm{x}_i^{(60)} = \left[
    \bm{x}_i^{(15)}\,\big\|\,
    \bar{\bm{x}}_{i,3}^{(15)}\,\big\|\,
    \bar{\bm{x}}_{i,5}^{(15)}\,\big\|\,
    \bar{\bm{x}}_{i,7}^{(15)}
  \right].
  \label{eq:featurevec}
\end{equation}
Figure~\ref{fig:diagram} contrasts this representation with per-cell
modelling. Spatial averages of the deployable column-type fields
(density, extinction) act as crude shielding estimators; this is why the
spatial features partially substitute for \(\fshield\) in the ablation of
Section~\ref{sec:ablation_fh2}.

\subsection{Density-weighted training}
\label{sec:weighting}

The cell population is dominated by diffuse gas: at the phase threshold
of Section~\ref{sec:metrics} the molecular fraction of cells ranges from
0.8 per cent (\(\Gzero = 6.4\)) to 68 per cent (\(\Gzero = 0.1\)), while the
H\(_2\) \emph{mass} resides almost entirely in the densest few per cent of
cells. An unweighted log-space loss therefore optimises the diffuse
majority and treats the mass-carrying tail as an afterthought. As a
designed counterweight we train each tabular model in two variants:
unweighted, and with smooth exponential sample weights
\begin{equation}
  w(y) = \exp\!\big[\ln(\alpha)\, t(y)\big], \qquad
  t(y) = \mathrm{clip}\!\left(
     \frac{y - q_{99}}{q_{99.99} - q_{99}}, 0, 1\right),
  \label{eq:weights}
\end{equation}
with \(\alpha = 100\) and \(q_p\) the \(p\)-th percentile of the training
targets, normalised to unit mean so the effective learning rate is
unchanged. The weight rises from \(1\times\) at the 99th percentile to
\(100\times\) at the 99.99th: the mass-carrying tail is upweighted without
destabilising the bulk fit. The percentile anchors and \(\alpha\) are a
designed choice, not tuned; their effect is reported in
Section~\ref{sec:results_main}.

\subsection{Models}
\label{sec:models}

\subsubsection{Gradient-boosted trees}
We use XGBoost \citep{Chen2016} with the GPU histogram method: 400
boosting rounds, maximum depth 6, learning rate 0.1, row subsampling
0.3, column subsampling 0.8. Depth variants 4 and 8 change the mean
\(\Rsq\) by less than 0.003 and were retired. Features are standardised
per training fold (a no-op for trees in principle; retained for pipeline
uniformity).

\subsubsection{Wide MLP}
The multilayer perceptron has hidden widths \([512, 512, 256, 128]\) with
batch normalisation \citep{Ioffe2015} and ReLU activations, trained with
Adam \citep{Kingma2015} (learning rate \(10^{-3}\), weight decay
\(10^{-5}\)), batch size 262,144, 100 epochs per fold, cosine-annealed
learning rate, and mixed precision. The full training fold is preloaded
to GPU memory and batched by on-device permutation. Predictions are
clipped to the training-target range \(\pm 2\dex\): the surrogate is not
permitted to invent densities far outside the training support (trees
have this property inherently, their leaf values being bounded by
training targets). Narrower and residual-block \citep{He2016} variants
give mean \(\Rsq\) within \(\sim\)0.02 with no consistent ordering across
runs; differences between MLP architectures are comparable to
seed-to-seed variation.

\subsubsection{3D U-Net}
\label{sec:cnn}
The volumetric baseline is a 3D U-Net \citep{Ronneberger2015,Cicek2016}
with three encoder stages, a bottleneck, trilinear-upsampling decoder,
skip connections, and residual convolution blocks, trained and evaluated
at the native \(128^3\) resolution (peak memory 5.7\,GiB at 32 base
channels and batch size 1 on an RTX 3090). Because each training sample
is one entire volume (batch size 1), instance normalisation
\citep{Ulyanov2017} replaces batch normalisation; note that instance
normalisation standardises each input volume by its own per-channel
statistics, giving the network a per-cube input adaptation at test time
that the tabular models do not receive (it uses input fields only, so no
target information is involved). Channel inputs and the training target
are standardised per fold from training-cube statistics; per-fold target
standardisation is necessary for stable training because the target mean
shifts by several dex from fold to fold (Section~\ref{sec:target}) and
otherwise dominates the loss. Training uses Adam (learning rate
\(5\times10^{-4}\), weight decay \(10^{-5}\)), gradient-norm clipping at
1.0, an unweighted mean-squared-error loss, and 150 epochs per fold.

Two protocol properties of the U-Net should be kept in mind when
reading its results. First, its reported checkpoint is selected per
fold by the loss on an inner validation cube drawn from the six
training cubes --- the cube closest to the median \(\log\Gzero\) of the
training set, a rule that keeps every fold's adjacent-\(\Gzero\)
neighbour in training --- so no held-out-cube information enters model
selection; the selection is nearly a no-op relative to simply taking
the final epoch (mean \(\Rsq\) 0.963 versus 0.962). The U-Net's numbers
are nonetheless sensitive to validation design: selecting instead the
training cube \emph{nearest} the held-out \(\Gzero\) removes the
boundary folds' only adjacent cube from training, turning those folds
into two-step extrapolation, and drops the mean \(\Rsq\) to 0.914.
Second, the U-Net output is unbounded ---
unlike the range-clamped MLP and the inherently bounded trees --- which
matters for its mass-budget behaviour
(Section~\ref{sec:results_cnn}). Convolutions use zero padding at the
box faces.

Data augmentation respects the broken symmetry of the illuminated cube:
of the 48 octahedral operations, only the eight \(z\)-preserving ones
(rotations about \(z\) and reflections through planes containing \(z\)) are
physically valid. Scalars transform by index permutation, velocities as
polar vectors, and the face-centred magnetic field components as axial
vectors with the appropriate face swaps. Using all 48 operations
injects unphysical samples and degrades validation accuracy. Each fold
therefore trains on \(6 \times 8 = 48\) augmented volumes; the held-out
cube is never augmented.

\subsection{Ensembles and bias calibration}
\label{sec:ensemble}

Two ensembles combine the spatial-feature XGBoost and MLP. The
\emph{equal-weight ensemble} averages their per-cell predictions. The
\emph{Ridge-stacked ensemble} fits a Ridge regression (\(\alpha = 1\))
meta-learner on out-of-fold (OOF) predictions of the training cubes and
applies it to the held-out cube. In the model-comparison experiments the
OOF predictions are reused from the outer cross-validation --- the
standard stacking shortcut, in which the base models that generate cube
\(j\)'s meta-training predictions were themselves trained with the
held-out cube in their training set; with a two-coefficient meta-learner
the indirect effect is small, but we do not describe those rows as fully
nested. The deployed pipeline of Section~\ref{sec:per_fold} instead uses
fully nested stacking: for each held-out cube, base models are retrained
on five training cubes to generate the OOF predictions, and final base
models on all six; the held-out cube is excluded throughout. The nested
pipeline scores slightly \emph{higher} than the shortcut rows, so the
comparison-table stacking numbers are, if anything, conservative. The
two base models have complementary inductive biases ---
piecewise-constant, conservative extrapolation for the trees; smooth
interpolation for the MLP --- and stacking them outperforms either alone
in every experiment. Both the unweighted pair and the density-weighted
pair (Section~\ref{sec:weighting}) are stacked, giving two stack
families.

\emph{Bias calibration.} The stacked predictions minimise squared error
but are not guaranteed unbiased on a held-out \(\Gzero\), and
Section~\ref{sec:massbudget} shows they are not. As a designed audit
step we therefore fit, for each held-out fold, the per-training-cube OOF
residual of the stacked model against \(\log_{10}\Gzero\) by two-parameter
least squares, and subtract the fitted value at the held-out \(\Gzero\)
from the predictions. Only training-cube quantities enter the fit; the
correction is a genuine out-of-sample prediction of the bias. The
residual functional can be chosen in two ways, and the choice is a
methodological decision we evaluate explicitly:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{mean calibration} centres the unweighted per-cube mean log
  residual --- the natural choice if all cells matter equally;
\item \emph{mass calibration} centres the mass-weighted mean residual
  \(b_M = \sum_i 10^{y_i}(\hat{y}_i - y_i) / \sum_i 10^{y_i}\)
  (per training cube), the constant-offset correction that
  approximately centres the predicted \emph{total H\(_2\) mass}, since
  the same dense cells dominate both the weights and the mass budget.
  Exact per-cube mass closure would instead subtract the log mass
  ratio \(\log_{10}(\sum_i 10^{\hat{y}_i} / \sum_i 10^{y_i})\); the two
  functionals coincide to first order in the residuals --- evaluated on
  the raw deployed volumes of Section~\ref{sec:per_fold} they differ by
  at most 0.018\dex{} (mass closure within 5 per cent) in every fold ---
  and the approximation is validated out of sample by the deployed
  pipeline's fold mass ratios of 0.985--1.090
  (Section~\ref{sec:per_fold}).
\end{itemize}
If the model's residual were independent of density the two would
coincide; their fitted offsets differ by 0.1--0.15\dex{} for the stacks
(Section~\ref{sec:massbudget}), and that gap is itself a useful
one-number diagnostic of density-dependent error. Each stack family is
reported raw, mean-calibrated, and mass-calibrated.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Main comparison}
\label{sec:results_main}

Tables~\ref{tab:main} and~\ref{tab:summary} report the
leave-one-\(\Gzero\)-out performance of all models on the full 15-feature
input set (plus 45 spatial features where indicated), and
Figure~\ref{fig:modelcomp} shows the per-fold behaviour. The progression
through the model families cleanly separates \emph{what each ingredient
buys}:

\emph{Spatial features buy precision.} Adding the 45 neighbourhood
features reduces XGBoost's RMSE from 0.49 to 0.37\dex{} and the MLP's
from 0.52 to 0.32\dex, and the improvement is almost entirely in the
scatter term of equation~(\ref{eq:rmse}): XGBoost's residual scatter
falls from 0.36 to 0.25\dex{} and the MLP's from 0.46 to 0.29\dex,
while the bias terms move by \(\la 0.1\dex\). Non-local context sharpens
the prediction; it does not recalibrate it.

\emph{Density weighting buys the mass budget --- for free.} The weighted
tree variant is statistically indistinguishable from the unweighted one
in \(\Rsq\) (0.963 in both cases; per-fold values agree to \(\pm 0.0003\))
yet its fold mass ratios move from 0.47--0.93 to 0.89--1.03: upweighting
the mass-carrying tail fixes \emph{where} the model is accurate without
degrading the bulk statistics. The weighted MLP behaves the same way
(mass ratios 0.92--1.03, versus 0.94--1.16 unweighted, with
\(\mathrm{MAE_{mw}}\) halved from 0.095 to 0.050\dex).

\emph{Stacking buys squared error and exposes a bias.} The Ridge stacks
have the lowest scatter in the comparison (\(s = 0.25\)--0.26\dex) but
carry a systematic \(+0.32\dex\) mean offset on the held-out cubes ---
the meta-learner's intercept zeroes the residual on the pooled training
folds, not on an unseen \(\Gzero\). Section~\ref{sec:massbudget} treats
this bias and its repair. After mass calibration the weighted stack is
the best model in the comparison: RMSE \(= 0.294\dex\) (the lowest of all
17 evaluated variants), \(\Rsq = 0.985 \pm 0.015\), mass ratios
0.93--1.07 in every fold, molecular-phase \(\Rsq = 0.991\), and skill
\(+0.55\) against the pointwise-XGBoost reference. We adopt it as the
final model. Two caveats attend this selection. With 17 variants,
seven folds, and several metrics, differences of
\(\la 0.01\) in mean \(\Rsq\) or \(\la 0.02\dex\) in RMSE lie within
fold-to-fold noise --- on RMSE the adopted model beats its
mean-calibrated counterpart in only 3 of 7 folds. The adoption
therefore rests on the criteria where the separations are large and
fold-consistent, not on the RMSE photo-finish: the mass-calibrated
weighted stack has the smaller absolute log mass error in 7 of 7 folds
against the mean-calibrated variant (ranges 0.93--1.07 versus
0.71--0.89) and beats the best single model on RMSE in 6 of 7 folds.

Two methodological observations from Table~\ref{tab:summary} are worth
recording because they generalise beyond this data set. First, the
\(\Rsq\) caveat of Section~\ref{sec:metrics} is not hypothetical: the
pointwise MLP \emph{beats} pointwise XGBoost on mean \(\Rsq\) (0.958
versus 0.950) while its pooled squared error is 18 per cent \emph{worse}
(skill \(-0.18\)) --- the MLP wins the high-variance low-\(\Gzero\) folds,
which mean \(\Rsq\) rewards. Second, \(W_1\) and the threshold fractions
expose a family difference that error moments hide: the trees are
moderately wrong almost everywhere (compressed predicted distribution;
\(f_{0.3} = 0.50\), \(W_1 = 0.30\)), whereas the MLP is nearly right for
most cells with a heavy error tail (\(f_{0.3} = 0.73\), \(W_1 = 0.22\),
despite the larger RMSE). The best marginal realism in the table belongs
to the weighted spatial MLP (\(W_1 = 0.109\)), slightly better than the
stacks --- Ridge averaging narrows the predicted distribution --- which
matters for applications that need statistically realistic fields
rather than minimum per-cell error (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).
The deployed nested pipeline recovers what the shortcut stacks lose
here, reaching a mean \(W_1\) of \(0.08\dex\)
(Section~\ref{sec:per_fold}).

\subsection{The H\texorpdfstring{\(_2\)}{2} mass budget and bias calibration}
\label{sec:massbudget}

The raw stacks illustrate why the mass budget is a primary metric. Their
mean residual on held-out cubes is \(+0.32\dex\) --- a factor of two in
density --- and the corresponding fold mass ratios are 1.08--1.65
(unweighted stack) and 1.65--1.84 (weighted stack): the
minimum-squared-error combination over-predicts the total H\(_2\) mass of
an unseen cube by up to 80 per cent while scoring \(\Rsq > 0.91\) in
every fold. The offset varies smoothly with the held-out \(\Gzero\),
which is exactly the structure the two-parameter calibration of
Section~\ref{sec:ensemble} is designed to remove; the fitted corrections
are \(+0.2\) to \(+0.4\dex\) depending on fold and functional.

The choice of residual functional matters, and
Table~\ref{tab:summary} quantifies the designed comparison.
\emph{Mean} calibration centres the cell-mean bias essentially perfectly
(\(b = -0.01\dex\)) and delivers the best skill (\(+0.60\)) --- but
\emph{overshoots} the mass budget, dragging the fold mass ratios down to
0.50--0.81 for the unweighted stack: the cell-mean offset is set by the
diffuse majority and is larger than the mass-weighted offset, so
subtracting it pushes the dense cells too low. \emph{Mass} calibration
centres the budget (\(\Mratio = 0.93\)--1.07 for the weighted stack) at
the cost of a \(+0.09\dex\) residual cell-mean bias carried by the diffuse
population. The two functionals differ because the stacks' residual
error is density-dependent --- a compression-to-the-mean pattern in
which dense cells are under-predicted relative to diffuse ones --- and
the 0.1--0.15\dex{} gap between the fitted offsets measures that
dependence directly. The two-parameter fit itself is stable: refitting
with each of the six training cubes removed in turn moves the applied
offset of the deployed pipeline by at most \(0.04\dex\) (equivalently
\(\la\)10 per cent in mass), the maximum occurring at the two boundary
folds, where the fit extrapolates in \(\log\Gzero\).
Under the primary-metric hierarchy (RMSE and mass
budget) the mass-calibrated weighted stack is the best configuration;
applications that weight all cells equally may prefer the
mean-calibrated variant, and Table~\ref{tab:summary} provides both.
Figure~\ref{fig:massbudget} shows the raw-versus-calibrated comparison
for the deployed pipeline of Section~\ref{sec:per_fold}, where the same
picture holds fold by fold.

\subsection{Dependence on the self-shielding factor}
\label{sec:ablation_fh2}

Because \(\fshield\) is produced by the solver the surrogate aims to
replace, Table~\ref{tab:fh2} repeats the comparison with \(\fshield\)
excluded from both the local features and the spatial averages (14
local + 42 spatial features). In aggregate the surrogate degrades
gracefully: the pointwise trees lose 0.039 in mean \(\Rsq\)
(0.950 \(\rightarrow\) 0.911), the pointwise MLP 0.049
(0.958 \(\rightarrow\) 0.909), the spatial variants roughly half as much
--- as expected if neighbourhood averages of density and extinction act
as crude column estimators --- and the mass-calibrated weighted stack
retains \(\Rsq = 0.964 \pm 0.014\) with its mass budget intact
(fold mass ratios 0.93--1.16).

The phase-resolved metrics tell a sharper story that the \(\Rsq\)
comparison alone would miss: for the final model the ablation raises the
RMSE by 60 per cent (0.294 \(\rightarrow\) 0.469\dex) and costs most of
the molecular-phase fidelity --- molecular \(\Rsq\) falls from 0.991 to
0.840 and the molecular bias grows from \(-0.01\) to \(-0.10\dex\). The
degradation concentrates precisely where H\(_2\) matters most: at
\(\Gzero = 0.1\), the most molecular cube, the final model's molecular
\(\Rsq\) drops to 0.70. The deployable (solver-independent) configuration
is thus a usable surrogate for aggregate statistics and morphology, but
applications requiring per-cell accuracy \emph{inside} the molecular
phase depend on a shielding estimate of \(\fshield\) quality.

Feature attribution corroborates the ablation.
Figure~\ref{fig:importance} shows XGBoost gain importances:
\(\log\fshield\) dominates (0.57 of total gain for the pointwise model;
0.83 when its spatial averages are aggregated with it), followed by
temperature, density, and \(\Gzero\); the velocity and magnetic-field
components contribute negligibly, suggesting a minimal six-feature
deployable set that we have not separately evaluated.

For the volumetric model the available evidence is an upper bound of a
different kind: a U-Net trained on only 11 inputs --- temperature,
\(\Gzero\), velocity, and magnetic field, i.e.\ \emph{no}
chemistry-adjacent quantities at all (no \(n_{\mathrm{H}}\),
\(n_{\mathrm{H^+}}\), extinction, or \(\fshield\)) --- still reaches
\(\Rsq = 0.851 \pm 0.121\). Roughly 85 per cent of the log-density
variance is thus recoverable from dynamics, thermodynamics, and the UV
strength alone: the H\(_2\) \emph{morphology} is largely inferable without
chemical inputs. The field-level metrics show what that number hides ---
residual scatter of 0.72\dex, only 15 per cent of cells within
0.1\dex, molecular-phase \(\Rsq\) near zero on average and negative at
both extrapolation folds, and fold mass ratios from 0.29 to a clipped
\(\times\)510 --- the large-scale morphology is retained, but chemically
precise molecular-phase prediction is lost.

\subsection{The volumetric baseline}
\label{sec:results_cnn}

The 3D U-Net occupies a revealing position in
Tables~\ref{tab:main}--\ref{tab:summary}: it is simultaneously the best
interpolator and the least reliable extrapolator under the
physical-field metrics. On the five
interior folds it is the most accurate model evaluated ---
\(\Rsq = 0.992\), RMSE \(0.22\dex\), beating the mass-calibrated weighted
stack (0.984, 0.31\dex) in aggregate --- direct evidence that a
learned volumetric representation extracts non-local information the
box-filter features do not. At the boundaries the picture inverts: mean
edge-fold \(\Rsq\) drops to 0.889 (RMSE 0.66\dex) against 0.988
(0.25\dex) for the calibrated stack, and against even the
\emph{pointwise} XGBoost baseline the U-Net's skill is \(-1.37\) at
\(\Gzero = 0.1\) and \(-0.01\) at \(\Gzero = 6.4\): outside its training
range the volumetric model does no better than the simplest tabular
one, and at the molecular-rich boundary substantially worse.

The physical-field metrics make the extrapolation failure qualitative,
not merely quantitative. At \(\Gzero = 6.4\) the U-Net's molecular-phase
bias is \(+1.13\dex\) and its fold mass ratio exceeds 290 (a lower bound;
its unbounded output is clipped at truth \(+1\dex\) before the mass
integral, Section~\ref{sec:metrics}); at \(\Gzero = 0.1\) it
under-predicts by \(-0.65\dex\) overall. Its interior-fold mass ratios
are also uncontrolled (0.71--19.0), consistent with its unweighted,
unbounded training. Crucially, this failure is \emph{not} repairable by
the calibration of Section~\ref{sec:ensemble}: the U-Net's held-out
bias is phase-localised (molecular \(+1.13\dex\) against a diffuse
\(+0.23\dex\) at \(\Gzero = 6.4\)) and wildly non-monotonic
in \(\Gzero\) (fold mass
ratios 1.7, 2.1, 1.1, 0.71, 1.8, 19.0, 291), whereas the stack's bias
is a global, \(\Gzero\)-smooth offset --- the difference between a
correctable systematic and a genuine extrapolation breakdown.

Two practical considerations reinforce the tabular default for suites of
this size. \emph{Configuration sensitivity}: in a dedicated
configuration study conducted at reduced (\(64^3\)) resolution, U-Net mean
\(\Rsq\) ranged from 0.80 to 0.97 across capacity variants (16, 32, 64
base channels; 1.5\,M--23\,M parameters) and training protocols, with a
dropout-regularised residual variant losing most of its accuracy at the
extrapolation folds (0.87/0.82 versus 0.90/0.95 for the dropout-free
baseline); with 48 augmented training volumes against millions of
parameters, training noise is large, and any single U-Net number carries
a wide implicit error bar. XGBoost results are bit-identical across
runs and MLP variation stays within \(\sim\)0.01. \emph{Cost}: the
seven-fold U-Net training takes \(\approx\)4.6\,h on an RTX 3090, versus
\(\approx\)1\,h for the \emph{entire} 17-variant tabular comparison
including spatial-feature construction. We did not pursue CNN-specific
remedies (density-weighted volumetric losses, deeper augmentation,
pretraining) and therefore make no claim about the ultimate capability
of volumetric architectures; the operational conclusion is that on a
suite of this size the engineered spatial features achieve comparable
interior accuracy with far greater out-of-distribution robustness at a
small fraction of the cost.

\subsection{Error analysis of the final model}
\label{sec:per_fold}

For per-cell and field-level analysis we use the deployed pipeline: the
density-weighted spatial stack with fully nested stacking and mass
calibration (Section~\ref{sec:ensemble}), which saves full \(128^3\)
prediction volumes for each held-out fold. Its per-fold \(\Rsq\) is
0.977, 0.988, 0.990, 0.987, 0.995, 0.996, 0.998 for
\(\Gzero = 0.1\)--6.4 (mean \(0.990 \pm 0.006\); RMSE \(0.25\dex\); the
nested protocol scores slightly above the comparison-table row, as
noted in Section~\ref{sec:ensemble}). The mass budget is closed in
every fold (\(\Mratio = 0.985\)--1.090), the molecular-phase \(\Rsq\) is
0.979--0.994, the cell-mean bias is \(+0.02\dex\), and the mass-weighted
MAE is 0.06\dex. The metric-level clip of Section~\ref{sec:metrics} is
never active for these volumes: no predicted cell falls outside the
held-out cube's true range \(\pm 1\dex\) in any fold, so all linear-space
quantities are computed from unclipped predictions. Within-fold sampling
uncertainty is small against the fold-to-fold spread: bootstrap 95 per
cent intervals (200 replicates per fold) span at most 0.0002 in per-fold
\(\Rsq\) when resampling cells and \(\la\)0.004 when resampling \(16^3\)
blocks to respect the field's spatial correlation --- an order of
magnitude below the 0.006 fold-to-fold standard deviation --- and the
per-fold mass-ratio intervals, the widest of any metric because the mass
integral is dominated by the densest cells, stay within 0.93--1.14 in
every fold. Fold-to-fold variation therefore dominates the uncertainty
of every cross-fold mean quoted in this paper. Figures~\ref{fig:massbudget}
and~\ref{fig:scatter}--\ref{fig:spatial} are derived from these
volumes.

Figure~\ref{fig:scatter} shows predicted versus true \(\logn\) for all
seven folds: the model reproduces the bimodal structure in every fold,
with scatter concentrated in the diffuse UV-exposed population and a
tight locus along the identity for the shielded population.
Figure~\ref{fig:residuals} shows the residual distributions: narrow
cores (widths 0.04--0.44\dex, shrinking monotonically with \(\Gzero\))
with per-fold means between \(-0.16\) and \(+0.16\dex\). The residual
structure identifies \(\Gzero = 0.8\) as the hardest fold for this
pipeline --- its bias (\(+0.16\dex\)) and its fraction of cells within
0.1\dex{} (38 per cent, against 60--99 per cent elsewhere) are the
weakest in the suite even though its \(\Rsq\) is 0.987 --- a reminder
that interior folds are not automatically easy.

\subsection{Single-cube transfer across \texorpdfstring{\(\Gzero\)}{G0}}
\label{sec:single_cube}

To measure how far the learned mapping transfers across the UV
parameter, we train the weighted spatial stack on each individual cube
and predict all seven, producing a \(7 \times 7\) matrix of log-space
\(\Rsq\) (Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap}). In-sample (diagonal) fits average
\(\Rsq = 0.997\). Transfer degrades systematically with \(\Gzero\)
separation: averaged over the matrix, one geometric step
(\(\times 2\) in \(\Gzero\)) gives \(\Rsq = 0.966\), two steps 0.888, three
steps 0.732, four steps 0.457, five steps 0.171, and the full 64-fold
separation \(-0.013\), with substantial spread between directions and cube
pairs at the larger separations. The mean off-diagonal \(\Rsq\) is 0.708.
The primary metrics decay in step: in-sample fits have
RMSE \(= 0.15\dex\) and total-mass ratios of 0.99--1.01; one geometric
step gives RMSE \(= 0.39\dex\) with mass ratios of 0.66--1.4; and four or
more steps reach RMSE \(= 1.3\)--\(1.7\dex\), biases up to \(\pm 1.3\dex\),
and mass ratios up to \(\sim\)5, bias and scatter growing together.
The learned local mapping is thus approximately universal within a
factor of \(\sim\)2--4 in \(\Gzero\) and degrades beyond it, which
(i) validates leave-one-\(\Gzero\)-out as a non-trivial protocol --- each
held-out cube is one step from its nearest training neighbour, so single-cube
knowledge alone would cap performance near 0.97, below what the six-cube
training sets achieve --- and (ii) confirms that none of the seven
\(\Gzero\) values is redundant in the training set.

\subsection{Within-cube sampling geometry}
\label{sec:intra_cube}

We next ask how reconstruction of a \emph{single} cube's \(\nHH\) field
depends on which cells are used for training --- a question about
sampling geometry in the simulation volume, not (we emphasise) a claim
about observational survey design, since real observations deliver
projected, beam-convolved, noisy quantities rather than simulation
fields, and \(\fshield\) is not an observable. For each cube we train the
weighted spatial stack on a subset of cells and evaluate on the
complement, comparing uniformly random subsets (1--75 per cent),
contiguous half-cube slabs along each axis, and compact cubic
sub-regions (1--50 per cent).

In these experiments the spatial features must respect the partial
coverage. Field volumes are masked before filtering: cells outside the
training subset are set to zero, and the box filter of
equation~(\ref{eq:boxfilter}) is applied to the masked volume,
\begin{equation}
  \bar{f}^{\mathrm{obs}}_{\bm{i},k} = \frac{1}{k^3}
    \sum_{\bm{j} \in \mathcal{N}_k(\bm{i})} m_{\bm{j}} f_{\bm{j}},
  \qquad
  m_{\bm{j}} =
  \begin{cases}
    1 & \bm{j} \in \text{training subset},\\
    0 & \text{otherwise},
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:maskedfilter}
\end{equation}
with \emph{no} renormalisation by the local sample count
\(\sum_j m_j\): a neighbourhood with no sampled cells yields zero, and a
sparsely sampled neighbourhood yields its sampled-cell sum diluted by
the full kernel volume. The local (unfiltered) features of test cells
use their true values; only the target labels and the neighbourhood
context are restricted to the training subset. This convention treats
the surrounding field values as unknown wherever they were not sampled;
it is deliberately conservative --- training cells near a subset
boundary also receive degraded context --- and its dilution property
matters for interpreting the sparse-coverage results below.

Figure~\ref{fig:intra_cube} and the run statistics give a consistent
picture across all seven cubes. Uniformly random coverage reconstructs
the field accurately from small budgets: at 10 per cent the stack
reaches a mean test \(\Rsq\) of 0.991 with RMSE \(= 0.24\dex\), 78 per cent
of cells within \(0.1\dex\), total-mass ratios of 0.82--1.23, and
molecular-phase \(\Rsq = 0.97\) (at 5 per cent: 0.983 and \(0.31\dex\); at
50 per cent: 0.994 and \(0.19\dex\)). Even at 1 per cent coverage, where
the expected number of sampled cells inside a \(7^3\) kernel is only
\(\sim\)3.4 and the diluted spatial features carry little signal, the
local features alone sustain a mean \(\Rsq\) of 0.90 (range 0.79--0.94
across cubes) --- although the primary metrics are less forgiving
there: RMSE \(= 0.69\dex\), 21 per cent of cells within \(0.1\dex\), and
mass ratios of 0.52--2.5.

Contiguous geometries illustrate why the metric hierarchy of
Section~\ref{sec:metrics} matters. Judged by log-space \(\Rsq\) alone
they merely pay a penalty: half-cube slabs --- 50 per cent of the
volume --- average \(\Rsq\) of 0.85, 0.89, 0.85 for \(x\), \(y\), \(z\), with
the worst cube--axis combination at 0.42, and compact boxes range from
0.84 (1 per cent) to 0.96 (\(\geq\)25 per cent). The primary metrics
reveal the failure that \(\Rsq\) forgives: slabs average
RMSE \(= 0.86\dex\) with only 7--18 per cent of cells within \(0.1\dex\),
molecular-phase \(\Rsq \leq 0.13\), and total-mass ratios from 0.006 to
4.4 --- the unsampled half contains physical regimes under-represented
in training, and those regimes are precisely the dense cells that carry
the mass. Small compact boxes are worse still, predicting essentially
zero total mass in their worst cubes. Geometry, not volume, is the
controlling variable: 1 per cent random coverage beats a 50 per cent
contiguous slab on every metric (\(\Rsq\) 0.90 versus 0.86;
RMSE 0.69 versus \(0.86\dex\)), and uniformly random sampling is the only
geometry tested that preserves the mass budget --- and then only at
\(\geq\)10 per cent coverage. We note that the MLP alone can fail
strongly on contiguous geometries (negative \(\Rsq\)); the Ridge stack
tracks the more robust XGBoost in those configurations.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Feature engineering versus architecture}

On this suite, input representation dominates architecture choice among
the tabular models. The spatial features cut the RMSE of both tabular
families by 23--38 per cent --- an improvement several times larger than
any architecture variation we tested (tree depth, MLP width and depth,
residual connections, all \(\la 0.01\) in \(\Rsq\) with no consistent
ordering) --- and stacking plus calibration buys a further \(\sim\)9 per
cent of RMSE over the best single model. This is consistent with the
broader observation that tree-based models with appropriate features
remain highly competitive on tabular problems \citep{Grinsztajn2022}.
The bias--scatter decomposition assigns each ingredient a distinct role:
spatial features reduce scatter only, calibration relocates bias only
(all three calibrated variants of a stack share the identical scatter by
construction), and density weighting selects \emph{which} functional of
the field is unbiased --- three orthogonal levers that compose into the
final model. The box-filter means are a deliberately minimal spatial
representation; anisotropic kernels aligned with the illumination axis,
or cumulative sums along it (a discrete column integral), are natural
refinements that we have not exhausted. A physics-based reference point
--- an analytic molecular-fraction prescription
\citep[e.g.][]{Krumholz2009,Gnedin2011,Bialy2016} evaluated
cell-by-cell on the suite --- would usefully anchor the ML numbers;
published formulae predict equilibrium molecular fractions from
column-averaged quantities under geometric assumptions the cell-level
task does not satisfy, so a fair comparison requires a calibration step
that we leave to future work.

\subsection{Volumetric versus per-cell representations}

The comparison sharpens into a clean division of regimes. Inside the
training range the U-Net is the best model evaluated --- interior-fold
\(\Rsq = 0.992\) against 0.984 for the calibrated stack --- so the learned
volumetric representation demonstrably extracts non-local structure the
box filters miss. Outside it, the U-Net fails in a way no global
correction can repair: phase-localised, non-monotonic in \(\Gzero\), with
mass-budget violations reaching two orders of magnitude, while the
stack's out-of-distribution error is a smooth, two-parameter-correctable
offset. The practical reading for limited-data suites is not
``tabular beats CNN'' but a division of labour: engineered neighbourhood
features plus calibration give robust extrapolation; the volumetric
model gives the best interpolation and should be preferred only within
a densely sampled parameter range. We also note the evaluation-scale
dependence of these statements: scoring the same stacked predictions
against a \(2\times2\times2\) box-averaged target raises its \(\Rsq\) from
0.962 to 0.991, i.e.\ roughly half of the residual error variance lives
at the smallest resolved scales, where cell-level structure is partly
unpredictable from the available inputs. Applications consuming
smoothed fields inherit the higher figure.

\subsection{Which model for which use}
\label{sec:whichmodel}

The metric hierarchy supports use-dependent recommendations rather than
a single winner. For physical fidelity of the predicted field --- mass
budgets, phase statistics --- the mass-calibrated weighted stack is the
clear choice (in the Table~\ref{tab:summary} comparison:
RMSE 0.29\dex, mass within 7 per cent, molecular \(\Rsq\) 0.991; its
deployed nested version reaches RMSE \(0.25\dex\) and mass within
9 per cent, Section~\ref{sec:per_fold}). For applications weighting
all cells equally, the mean-calibrated stack minimises cell-mean error
(bias \(-0.01\dex\), skill \(+0.60\)) at the price of a 20--50 per cent
mass deficit. For generating statistically realistic \(\nHH\) fields
(synthetic observations, sub-grid realisations) the deployed nested
pipeline is also the best option (mean \(W_1 = 0.08\dex\)); among the
single models the weighted spatial MLP is the strongest
(\(W_1 = 0.109\dex\)), and the shortcut stacks are slightly worse
because Ridge averaging narrows the predicted distribution. And for
interpolation-only use within a well-sampled parameter range, the
volumetric U-Net is the most accurate option available.

\subsection{Deployability and the shielding factor}
\label{sec:deploy}

The ablation of Section~\ref{sec:ablation_fh2} addresses the most
delicate methodological question in this study: whether the surrogate's
accuracy is borrowed from the solver through \(\fshield\). The answer is
use-dependent. For aggregate statistics the dependence is modest: the
solver-independent final model retains \(\Rsq = 0.964\) with a closed
mass budget, because neighbourhood averages of the deployable
column-type fields substitute for much of the shielding information.
For per-cell accuracy inside the molecular phase the dependence is
strong (molecular \(\Rsq\) \(0.991 \rightarrow 0.840\), and 0.70 in the
most molecular cube): resolving \emph{which} cells cross the
atomic-to-molecular transition requires genuine shielding information.
Deployment therefore has a spectrum of options: with a cheap
approximate shielding estimate (e.g.\ from extinction), accuracy should
fall between the two blocks of Table~\ref{tab:fh2}; with no shielding
estimate at all, the without-\(\fshield\) block applies directly. What
our data cannot quantify is the effect of using an \emph{inconsistent}
shielding estimator (one with different systematics from the solver's),
which would require forward-modelling the estimator itself.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\textit{Dataset scope.} The seven simulations grow from one turbulent
initial realisation and differ only in their imposed \(\Gzero\), so their
structures are strongly correlated even though the evolved fields are
not identical (Section~\ref{sec:suite}). All generalisation claims are
therefore claims about the UV-field axis; transfer across turbulence
realisations, metallicities, dust models, or resolutions is untested and
plausibly harder. The suite is also quasi-equilibrium and uniformly
illuminated; time-dependent or anisotropic radiation fields would
invalidate the \(z\)-preserving augmentation and may change the role of
the spatial features.

\textit{Simulation provenance.} This paper characterises surrogate
methodology on a controlled private suite, and its conclusions are
internal to that suite (relative model performance, feature ablations,
sampling geometry). Application of the trained surrogate to scientific
inference requires the full simulation specification --- code, chemical
network, box size, resolution, metallicity, dust-to-gas ratio, driving
--- which is not part of this study.

\textit{Boundary treatment.} The spatial features reflect
neighbourhoods at the box faces and the U-Net zero-pads its
convolutions; cells within three voxels of a face (\(\approx\)13 per cent
of the volume at the largest kernel) therefore receive approximate
context. Matching the treatment to the simulations' true boundary
conditions is a straightforward refinement we have not evaluated.

\textit{CNN comparison.} The U-Net was trained with an unweighted loss
and an unbounded output, and receives per-cube input normalisation at
test time through its instance-normalisation layers --- a mixture of
disadvantages and advantages relative to the tabular protocol. Its
checkpoint selection is leakage-free (inner validation cube,
Section~\ref{sec:cnn}), but the choice of validation cube visibly moves
its numbers (mean \(\Rsq\) 0.914--0.963 across the two selection rules
we evaluated). A density-weighted,
range-bounded U-Net would be the fairer mass-budget comparison and is
left to future work; the configuration-sensitivity results suggest any
single U-Net number carries a wide error bar regardless.

\textit{Calibration hyperparameters.} The density-weighting anchors
(\(q_{99}\), \(q_{99.99}\), \(\alpha = 100\)) are a designed choice whose
sensitivity we have not mapped; the calibration itself is a
two-parameter linear model in \(\log\Gzero\) and inherits the smoothness
assumption it encodes. The residual \(+0.16\dex\) bias of the deployed
model at \(\Gzero = 0.8\) (Section~\ref{sec:per_fold}) marks the
limit of what a global linear correction captures on this suite.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conc}

We evaluated machine-learning surrogates for the molecular hydrogen
number density in a controlled suite of seven \(128^3\) UV-only ISM
chemistry simulations under leave-one-\(\Gzero\)-out cross-validation,
scoring every model on the same native \(128^3\) cells with a metric
hierarchy built around the physical uses of the predicted field.
The main conclusions are:

(i) How a surrogate is scored determines what one learns about it.
Models separated by \(\la\)0.03 in log-space \(\Rsq\) differ by factors of
several in recovered H\(_2\) mass; the minimum-squared-error stacked
ensemble carries a \(+0.32\dex\) held-out bias (mass over-prediction up
to 80 per cent) that \(\Rsq\) does not register. RMSE decomposed into
bias and scatter, the mass budget, and phase-conditional errors should
be standard reporting for field-level emulators.

(ii) The ingredients of the final model act on distinct error
components: multi-scale box-filter features (computed in
\(\approx\)30\,s) cut \emph{scatter} by roughly a third; density-weighted
training closes the pointwise models' \emph{mass budgets} at zero
\(\Rsq\) cost; and a two-parameter, mass-weighted bias calibration in
\(\log\Gzero\), fitted on training cubes alone, removes the stack's
\emph{bias}. The deployed pipeline reaches RMSE \(= 0.25\dex\),
\(\Rsq = 0.990 \pm 0.006\), total H\(_2\) mass within 9 per cent, and
molecular-phase \(\Rsq \ge 0.98\) on every held-out fold.

(iii) The choice of calibration functional is a physical decision, not
a technicality: centring the cell-mean error overshoots the mass budget
by 20--50 per cent because the surrogate's residuals are
density-dependent; centring the mass-weighted residual closes the
budget at the cost of a \(+0.09\dex\) diffuse-phase offset. We recommend
reporting both.

(iv) The solver-derived self-shielding factor is dispensable for
morphology but not for molecular-phase chemistry: without it the final
model retains \(\Rsq = 0.964\) and a closed mass budget, but its
molecular-phase \(\Rsq\) falls from 0.991 to 0.840 (0.70 in the most
molecular cube) and its RMSE grows by 60 per cent.

(v) The H\(_2\) \emph{morphology} is largely inferable without any
chemistry-adjacent input: a U-Net given only temperature, velocity,
magnetic field, and \(\Gzero\) reaches \(\Rsq = 0.851\) --- yet with
\(0.72\dex\) scatter, near-zero molecular-phase \(\Rsq\), and fold mass
ratios from 0.29 to a clipped \(\times\)510. The gap between that number
and the full-input models quantifies how much of the surrogate's skill
is chemistry rather than memorised density structure.

(vi) The volumetric 3D U-Net is the best interpolator in the comparison
(interior-fold \(\Rsq = 0.992\), beating every tabular model in
aggregate) and the
least reliable extrapolator under the physical-field metrics: at the
boundary folds it does no better than the pointwise XGBoost baseline
(and at the molecular-rich boundary substantially worse),
with phase-localised biases up to \(+1.1\dex\) and
mass-budget violations exceeding two orders of magnitude that a global
calibration cannot repair --- at roughly two orders of magnitude higher
per-model training cost. On suites of this size, engineered spatial context plus
calibration is the robust default; the volumetric model is preferable
only within a densely sampled parameter range.

(vii) The learned local mapping transfers across a factor of
\(\sim\)2--4 in \(\Gzero\) (single-cube transfer \(\Rsq = 0.966\) at one
geometric step, 0.888 at two) and degrades beyond it, validating
leave-one-\(\Gzero\)-out as a non-trivial protocol and confirming that
none of the seven simulations is redundant.

(viii) Within a cube --- in a sparse-label setting where cell-local
inputs are available everywhere but labels and neighbourhood context
are restricted to the training sample --- reconstruction quality is
controlled by sampling geometry rather than sample volume: 1 per cent
uniformly random
coverage achieves \(\Rsq = 0.90\), matching 50 per cent contiguous slabs
(\(\Rsq = 0.86\) on average), and the primary metrics widen the gap ---
contiguous geometries retain acceptable log-space \(\Rsq\) but lose
molecular-phase fidelity and corrupt the mass budget by up to two
orders of magnitude, while random coverage of 10 per cent or more
preserves all metrics. These are simulation-space
results;
translating them to observational survey design would require forward
modelling of observables.

The full tabular pipeline --- feature construction, training,
calibration, and prediction for all seven folds --- runs in about an
hour on a single consumer GPU, and the trained surrogate predicts all
2,097,152 cells of a new cube in seconds. The per-cube cost of the
suite's non-equilibrium solver on comparable hardware is not available
to us, so the speed-up over \emph{this} solver is not quantified; the
surrogate cost alone is reported.
